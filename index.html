PSALMS AND MEDITATIONS

— Psalm

“A man cannot lose either the past or the future: for what a man has not, how can anyone take this from him?”

Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, Book II, Verse 14

When two principles oppose each other, there must be a third which encapsulates both in one whole. This third principle gives context to the other two and distinguishes them from each other. In the case of time, past and future are not only ideas in themselves, they are also two ways of experiencing the present. It is only through the present that the future can be altered and the past written, because it is the only moment in time when we can act. It is at this moment that all our living must take place.

The Book of Psalms operates in a similar way. Psalms are often placed into two categories: sorrow and praise. However, rather than being two distinct groups, they are a spectrum from sorrow to praise, and somewhere between them is a point where sorrow becomes praise, and supplication becomes commitment. At this point, sorrow and praise operate together, both encapsulated in the idea of faith.

To create this artwork, I categorized each psalm, arranging them along two axes. The horizontal axis represents the shift from sorrow to praise and the vertical axis represents the shift from supplication to commitment. I created symbols to capture the central idea of each psalm, but the diagram is meant to be understood as a whole, not as a collection of individual tiles. Somewhere between past and future, sorrow and praise, supplication and commitment is the moment when all meet in a single act of devotion to God, a complete expression of faith.
