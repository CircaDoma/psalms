Psalms and Meditations
ABOUT

“A man cannot lose either the past or the future: for what a man has not, how can anyone take this from him? This thing thou must bear in mind, that he who will live longest and he who will die soonest lose just the same. For the present is the only thing of which a man can be deprived.”

- Marcus Aurelius, The Meditations

When two principles perfectly oppose each other, a third exists above and beyond them, containing both within it. This third principle is the context and true identity of the two.

Consider TIME. Past and future do not exist in themselves. They are instead perceptions of the present. Only through the present is the future altered and the past written. Only in this moment of time can any action take place. This fraction of an instant contains all that has ever been and all that will ever be. The long, unending, enduring present.

Consider POSITION. The individual places themselves in relation to God in only two ways. He glorifies him in his peace, and he laments of his anguish. Joy and sorrow war within the individual, each seeking to expel the other, and yet pure, earnest connection, does not distinguish the two, but fuses them together. A single act of petition and praise. A unified voice, lamenting the world and rejoicing in him who transcended it.

Consider DIRECTION. The momentum of a disciple’s trajectory is fundamentally distinct from their position at any point in time. It is therefore impossible to discern one’s direction from their position. The disciple’s direction can be subdivided into two modes of action: supplication and commitment. The first asks, the second answers. The first seeks, the second finds. The first draws him to God, the second draws him to his neighbor. Both must exist in the saint, and neither can exist in the devil.

To create this work, I ordered each psalm along the two axes of position and direction. As the placement of the psalm is my statement on its general context, the image for each psalm is my statement on its specific context. Each psalm can be observed as an individual, but they must be understood as a whole. Like the enduring present, they can only be subdivided by arbitrary action, not by inherent delineation. And somewhere between past and future, sorrow and praise, supplication and commitment lies the moment when all meet in a single act of devotion to God, a complete expression of faith.
